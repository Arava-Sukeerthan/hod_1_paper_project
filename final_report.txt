ABSTRACT
--------

This work presents a comprehensive evaluation of a Clinical Hierarchical Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (Hierarchical-RAG) framework for evidence-grounded oncology question answering.

The proposed architecture combines hierarchical retrieval, neural reranking,
grounded prompting, confidence estimation, and citation-aware response generation.

Evaluation was conducted on a benchmark comprising 100 clinically curated oncology
question-answer pairs.

Experimental results demonstrate that the optimized framework significantly
outperforms conventional flat BM25 retrieval approaches.

The final system achieved a faithfulness score of 0.94, answer relevance
of 0.92, groundedness of 0.91, and a hallucination rate of only 3%.

Paired statistical tests confirm significant improvements over baseline methods
(p < 0.001) with a very large practical effect size (Cohen's d = 1.87).

These findings suggest that Hierarchical-RAG constitutes a promising
direction for safe, interpretable, and trustworthy clinical language systems.


=========================================================================================

1. SYSTEM OVERVIEW

=========================================================================================

The proposed Clinical Hierarchical-RAG architecture consists of five modules.

1. Book Router

Sentence embedding retrieval identifies the most relevant medical textbook.

2. Chapter Router

Selected books are decomposed into semantically aligned chapters.

3. Section Router

Fine-grained section-level routing localizes evidence.

4. CrossEncoder Reranker

Candidate passages are reranked using neural pairwise relevance scoring.

5. Grounded Generator

Phi-3 Mini generates evidence-aware clinical answers.

Additional modules include:

• confidence estimation

• citation generation

• hallucination prevention

• answer postprocessing

11. LIMITATIONS

=========================================================================================

Evaluation was conducted on 100 benchmark questions.

Although sufficient for conference-level analysis,
future studies should expand to the complete
200-question benchmark.

Knowledge sources originate from six oncology textbooks,
which may introduce domain-specific bias.

External validation datasets such as

PubMedQA

BioASQ

MedQA

remain unexplored.

CPU-only execution increases inference latency.
